Every few years, the enterprise technology pendulum swings — from building everything in-house to buying everything off the shelf. Today, AI is adding weight to both sides, and most companies are getting the decision wrong.

According to Forrester's 2025 Digital Workplace Survey, 39% of organizations are exploring generative AI use cases to improve productivity, but adoption is inconsistent and value realization remains elusive. Meanwhile, 43% of IT leaders report that improving end-user adoption is their top priority — a clear sign that off-the-shelf solutions aren't meeting real business needs.

The old playbook doesn't work anymore. SaaS gave us speed, but AI demands strategy. Here's how to make the right build vs buy decision for your business.

Why the Traditional Build vs Buy Framework Is Broken

For decades, the build vs buy decision was relatively straightforward. You built when commercial solutions didn't exist, and bought when they did. Speed and cost predictability usually won.

AI has shattered this logic. McKinsey's 2026 research found that "AI changes too fast for multiyear build cycles, and re-creating foundational layers can drain resources without creating differentiation."

But off-the-shelf AI tools have their own problems:

  • Generic solutions for unique workflows: Most AI tools are built for the lowest common denominator, not your specific business processes
  • Vendor roadmap dependency: Your strategic priorities don't align with their revenue goals
  • Integration complexity: Nearly 70% of employees face too many applications and constant context switching
  • Escalating costs: AI pricing models are often usage-based and unpredictable at scale
  • Limited customization: The features you need most are often impossible or expensive to modify

The result? Forrester's data shows organizations are stuck with AI implementations that deliver "meh" on outcomes while consuming significant resources.

The New Decision Framework: Strategic vs Commodity

The question isn't "build or buy?" anymore. It's "Where can AI create competitive advantage, and where is it just table stakes?"

Based on research from Gartner and McKinsey, here's the strategic framework that works:

Buy Off-the-Shelf When:

1. The Use Case Is Standardized

Customer support chatbots, email marketing automation, basic document processing — these are solved problems. According to Gartner's enterprise AI research, "for standardized applications such as customer support automation or reporting tools, pre-built solutions are often adopted to ensure efficiency and reduce development overhead."

2. Speed to Market Is Critical

When you need AI capabilities deployed in weeks, not months, commercial solutions win. The time cost of building often outweighs the customization benefits.

3. Internal Expertise Is Limited

If your team doesn't have deep AI/ML capabilities, buying gives you access to specialized knowledge without the hiring and training overhead.

4. Regulatory Compliance Is Complex

Enterprise AI vendors have already solved compliance for GDPR, HIPAA, SOC 2. Building compliant AI from scratch is expensive and risky.

Build Custom When:

1. Your Workflow Is Your Competitive Advantage

If how you do something is what differentiates you in the market, generic AI tools will commoditize that advantage. McKinsey's research emphasizes building "where domain-specific logic or proprietary workflows create real competitive advantage."

2. Data Is Your Moat

If your competitive advantage comes from proprietary data or unique data relationships, custom AI that leverages that data strategically will outperform generic tools every time.

3. Integration Requirements Are Complex

When AI needs to orchestrate across multiple internal systems in ways that create new workflows, custom development often delivers better ROI than trying to force-fit multiple tools together.

4. Total Cost of Ownership Favors Building

For high-volume use cases, usage-based pricing for commercial AI tools can become prohibitively expensive. Building may cost more upfront but deliver better unit economics at scale.

The Hybrid Approach: Buy Foundational, Build Differentiating

The most successful AI implementations aren't purely buy or build — they're strategic combinations that buy standardized capabilities and build competitive differentiation.

Here's what this looks like in practice:

Foundation Layer: Buy

  • LLM access (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google)
  • Vector databases (Pinecone, Weaviate)
  • ML infrastructure (AWS SageMaker, Google Vertex AI)
  • Standard integrations (Zapier, pre-built API connectors)

Application Layer: Build

  • Custom workflows that match your business processes
  • Proprietary data integration that creates unique insights
  • Domain-specific UI/UX that reduces friction for your users
  • Business logic orchestration that automates your competitive advantages

This approach gives you the speed of commercial solutions with the strategic value of custom development.

The Hidden Costs of Each Approach

Off-the-Shelf Hidden Costs:

  • Integration complexity: Making different tools work together often costs more than the tools themselves
  • Change management: Training users on multiple interfaces with different paradigms
  • Vendor lock-in: Switching costs that increase over time
  • Feature creep: Paying for capabilities you don't need
  • Usage unpredictability: AI pricing models can create budget surprises

Custom Build Hidden Costs:

  • Ongoing maintenance: Models drift, APIs change, infrastructure needs updates
  • Expertise retention: Key people leave, taking institutional knowledge with them
  • Security and compliance: You own all the risk
  • Technology debt: Custom solutions can become harder to maintain over time
  • Opportunity cost: Engineering resources that could be building customer-facing features

Making the Decision: A Practical Checklist

For each AI initiative, ask these questions:

Strategic Assessment:

  • Does this AI capability differentiate us competitively, or is it table stakes?
  • Do we have unique data or workflows that would make custom AI significantly better?
  • Is this core to our business model, or is it a supporting process?

Resource Assessment:

  • Do we have the AI/ML expertise to build and maintain this internally?
  • Can we commit to ongoing development and maintenance?
  • What's our tolerance for technical risk and complexity?

Economic Assessment:

  • What's the 3-year total cost of ownership for both options?
  • How predictable are the costs for each approach?
  • What's the opportunity cost of the engineering resources required?

Timeline Assessment:

  • How quickly do we need this capability in production?
  • Can we afford the additional development time for custom solutions?
  • Is this blocking other strategic initiatives?

When to Change Your Mind

Unlike traditional software, AI requires continuous reassessment of build vs buy decisions. McKinsey's research emphasizes that "unlike the traditional SaaS world, AI requires a continuous, deliberate reassessment of what to create and what to consume."

Triggers to reconsider your approach:

  • Scale changes: What made sense at 100 users may not at 10,000
  • Capability maturity: Commercial solutions improve, or new options emerge
  • Strategic shifts: Business priorities change, affecting what's core vs commodity
  • Cost structures evolve: Pricing models change, or usage patterns shift dramatically
  • Competitive landscape: What differentiates you today may be table stakes tomorrow

Key Takeaways

  • The old build vs buy framework is broken for AI — speed and cost aren't the only factors that matter
  • Buy for standardized use cases where speed to market and proven solutions outweigh customization needs
  • Build for competitive differentiation where your unique data, workflows, or business logic create strategic advantage
  • Hybrid approaches often work best — buy foundational capabilities, build differentiating applications
  • Hidden costs are significant for both approaches — factor in integration, maintenance, and opportunity costs
  • Reassess continuously — AI evolves too fast for one-time decisions
  • Strategy first, technology second — start with what creates competitive advantage, then choose the right implementation approach

The organizations that master this strategic approach to build vs buy will turn AI complexity into competitive advantage. The ones that default to "just buy something" will find themselves with expensive tool sprawl and minimal business impact.
