The average company now runs 275 SaaS applications. Large enterprises use 660. SaaS spending is growing faster than portfolios — costs are up while the number of apps barely budges. And yet teams still complain they can't find a tool that does exactly what they need.

Something has shifted. According to Retool's 2026 Build vs. Buy report, 35% of teams have already replaced at least one SaaS tool with a custom build, and 78% expect to build more custom internal tools this year. The build-vs-buy equation hasn't just tilted — it's flipped.

This post breaks down why it's happening, how companies are actually doing it, and what separates the teams that ship useful internal tools from those that end up with expensive science projects.

Why the SaaS Model Is Cracking

SaaS was supposed to simplify everything. Subscribe, log in, done. But after a decade of buying, companies are drowning in tool sprawl. Zylo's 2025 SaaS Management Index found that smaller companies (under 500 employees) spend an average of $11.5 million on SaaS, while large enterprises average $284 million. That spending is rising faster than the value being extracted.

The problems compound:

  • Data silos everywhere. Your CRM doesn't talk to your project management tool, which doesn't talk to your billing system. Every integration is another failure point.
  • Forced workflows. SaaS products impose their own opinions about how work should flow. Your team adapts to the tool instead of the tool adapting to your team.
  • Pricing that punishes growth. Per-seat pricing means every new hire is another line item across a dozen subscriptions. Vendors increasingly shift to consumption-based and AI-premium pricing tiers that are difficult to predict.
  • Shadow IT explosion. When official tools don't fit, people build workarounds. Retool's survey found that 60% of respondents built software outside IT oversight in the past year.

The result is a paradox: companies spend more on software every year and still can't get the tools they actually need.

Why "Build" Is Suddenly Viable

Building internal tools used to mean six-month projects, dedicated engineering teams, and maintenance nightmares. That's no longer the case, and three forces are driving the change.

1. AI-Assisted Development Has Collapsed Build Times

The most dramatic shift is in development speed. AI coding assistants, agent-based development workflows, and low-code platforms with AI integration mean that a single developer (or even a technical ops person) can ship a working internal tool in days, not months.

Retool's survey backs this up: 51% of builders have already shipped production software using AI assistance, and about half of those report saving six or more hours per week. When the cost of building drops by an order of magnitude, the math changes for every tool evaluation.

2. AI Makes Custom Tools Genuinely Smarter Than Off-the-Shelf

This is the key insight most companies miss. The value of building isn't just cost savings — it's capability. A custom internal tool can:

  • Query your proprietary data with natural language using RAG (retrieval-augmented generation)
  • Automate multi-step workflows that span your specific systems
  • Make decisions based on your business logic, not a vendor's generic rules engine
  • Improve continuously based on how your team actually uses it

No SaaS vendor can build something that understands your deal pipeline, your content taxonomy, and your fulfillment process simultaneously. But a custom AI tool built on your data can.

3. The Agent Paradigm Changes What "Internal Tool" Means

Gartner predicts that 40% of enterprise applications will include task-specific AI agents by 2026, up from less than 5% in 2025. That's not a gradual shift — it's a phase change.

Internal tools are no longer just dashboards and forms. They're autonomous agents that monitor systems, flag anomalies, draft reports, triage tickets, and execute workflows with minimal human input. McKinsey's research on agentic AI makes this point directly: while 78% of companies have implemented some form of generative AI, most deployments haven't moved the P&L because they're stuck on horizontal tools (chatbots, copilots) rather than vertical, process-specific agents.

The companies seeing real returns are the ones building AI agents tailored to their core business processes — not plugging ChatGPT into Slack and calling it AI adoption.

What Companies Are Actually Building

Based on what we see across our client work and the broader market, the highest-impact custom AI tools fall into a few categories:

Internal Operations Tools

  • Automated reporting dashboards that pull from multiple systems (CRM, project management, finance) and generate narrative summaries — not just charts
  • Workflow automation engines that replace the Zapier/Make duct tape with purpose-built orchestration tied to your specific business rules
  • Knowledge bases that actually work — RAG-powered search across internal docs, Slack history, wikis, and meeting transcripts

Customer-Facing Internal Tools

  • AI-powered support triage that classifies, routes, and drafts responses based on your product's actual documentation and past resolutions
  • Sales intelligence tools that enrich leads using your specific ICP criteria, not a vendor's generic scoring model
  • Content operations platforms that manage creation, review, and distribution across your specific CMS, channels, and approval workflows

Data and Analytics Tools

  • Natural language querying over your data warehouse — "What was our churn rate by segment last quarter?" returns an answer, not a SQL editor
  • Anomaly detection agents that monitor metrics and proactively surface issues before someone notices a dashboard going red
  • Client health scoring that synthesizes signals from calls, support tickets, usage data, and billing into a single actionable view

The Practical Playbook: How to Start Building

If your team is considering building AI-powered internal tools, here's the approach that consistently works.

Step 1: Audit Your SaaS Stack for Build Candidates

Not every SaaS tool should be replaced. Look for tools where:

  • You use less than 30% of the features but pay for 100%
  • The tool forces you into workarounds for your actual workflow
  • Multiple teams use different tools that do overlapping things
  • The tool sits between two systems and mostly just moves data

Workflow automations and internal admin tools lead the replacement list in Retool's survey, followed by CRMs, BI tools, project management, and customer support. Start where the pain is loudest.

Step 2: Start With a Single Workflow, Not a Platform

The biggest mistake is trying to build a comprehensive platform on day one. Instead, pick one specific workflow that's painful, manual, or spread across too many tools. Build a tool that handles that one thing well. Ship it. Get feedback. Iterate.

A client health scorecard that pulls from three systems and generates a weekly summary is a better first project than "an AI-powered everything dashboard."

Step 3: Choose Your Stack Deliberately

The technology choices matter less than people think, but they do matter. For most internal AI tools, you need:

  • A frontend framework — React, Next.js, or even a low-code tool like Retool for pure internal use
  • An API layer — Node.js, Python/FastAPI, or serverless functions
  • An LLM integration — direct API access to models (OpenAI, Anthropic, open-source), not just a chatbot wrapper
  • A data layer — your existing databases, plus potentially a vector store for RAG
  • An orchestration layer — for multi-step AI workflows, something like LangChain, Temporal, or custom agent frameworks

The key decision is whether to build on a low-code/AppGen platform or go fully custom. Low-code is faster for standard CRUD tools; custom code wins when you need deep AI integration, complex business logic, or specific performance characteristics.

Step 4: Invest in the Boring Parts

The exciting part is the AI. The important part is everything else: authentication, error handling, logging, monitoring, and graceful degradation when the model returns something unexpected. AI responses are non-deterministic by nature. Your internal tool needs to handle that gracefully — with validation, fallbacks, and human-in-the-loop checkpoints where stakes are high.

Step 5: Measure and Iterate

Track adoption and impact from day one. How many people use the tool daily? How much time does it save per task? What's the error rate? These metrics justify further investment and guide iteration. Retool's data shows that 75% of builders now work under an AI directive, but 35% of organizations still haven't established AI productivity metrics. Don't be in that 35%.

How We Think About This at Last Rev

We've spent years building digital platforms for mid-market and enterprise clients — composable architectures, headless CMS implementations, high-performance web applications. AI-powered internal tools are a natural extension of that work.

Our approach is opinionated:

  • Build for your actual workflows, not hypothetical ones. We start by understanding how your team actually works — the real process, not the documented one. The tool fits the team, not the other way around.
  • Use AI where it adds leverage, not everywhere. Not every screen needs an AI feature. A simple CRUD form is still the right answer for simple data entry. AI earns its place when it reduces cognitive load, automates judgment calls, or connects dots across systems.
  • Ship fast, then harden. Internal tools should be in users' hands within weeks, not months. But "fast" doesn't mean "fragile." We build in observability, error handling, and feedback loops from the start.
  • Own your data and your logic. Custom doesn't mean isolated. We build tools that integrate with your existing systems through clean APIs — but the business logic and data stay under your control, not locked inside a vendor's platform.

The shift McKinsey describes — from horizontal AI experiments to vertical, process-specific agents — is exactly what we're building with our clients. Not generic chatbots. Not AI features bolted onto existing SaaS. Purpose-built tools that solve the specific problems your team faces every day.

Key Takeaways

  • SaaS sprawl is a real cost center. The average company runs 275 apps and spends millions, yet still has workflow gaps that no vendor fills.
  • Building is no longer prohibitively expensive. AI-assisted development, low-code platforms, and modern frameworks have collapsed build times from months to weeks or days.
  • Custom AI tools are smarter than generic SaaS. Tools built on your data, your business logic, and your workflows deliver capabilities no off-the-shelf product can match.
  • Start small and specific. Replace one painful workflow, not your entire tool stack. Measure impact. Expand from there.
  • The agent era is here. 40% of enterprise apps will feature AI agents by end of 2026. Companies building now will have a structural advantage over those still buying.

The question isn't whether to build AI-powered internal tools. It's which ones to build first.
