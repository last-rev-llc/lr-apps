Nearly 60% of AI leaders say their biggest challenge with agentic AI is integrating with legacy systems. Not model selection. Not prompt engineering. Integration.

That tracks with what we see in practice. The AI part of an AI app is rarely the hard part. The hard part is connecting it to the CRM that runs on a 10-year-old API, the ERP that exports CSV files at 2 AM, and the internal tool that only one person understands. Most teams that fail at AI don't fail at AI — they fail at systems integration.

This post walks through the actual process for building an AI application that plugs into your existing stack, phase by phase. No theory. No "it depends." The real steps, the real decisions, and where things actually go wrong.

Phase 1: Map Your Systems Before You Touch a Model

The most common mistake is starting with the AI model and working backward. "We'll use GPT-4 to…" is the wrong first sentence. The right first sentence is: "Here's what our systems do today, and here's the gap."

Before writing a line of code, you need a systems inventory:

  • What systems hold the data the AI needs? CRM, ERP, data warehouse, content management system, support ticketing, billing — list them all.
  • What are the integration surfaces? REST APIs, GraphQL, webhooks, database connections, file exports, SFTP drops. Be specific. "It has an API" is not enough — you need to know rate limits, auth mechanisms, data formats, and whether the API actually exposes the fields you need.
  • Where does data flow today? Draw the current data flow. You'll almost certainly find that critical data lives in three places, two of which are stale, and the authoritative source requires a VPN.
  • Who owns each system? The technical owner, the business owner, and the person who will panic when they see unfamiliar API calls in their logs.

This isn't busywork. According to Deloitte's 2026 State of AI in the Enterprise report, the AI skills gap is the single biggest barrier to integration — but that gap isn't about knowing how to prompt an LLM. It's about understanding how data actually moves through an organization and where it breaks down.

Phase 2: Define the Integration Architecture

Once you know what you're working with, you need to decide how the AI app connects to everything else. There are three common patterns, and the right choice depends on your constraints.

Pattern 1: API-First (Direct Integration)

Your AI app calls existing system APIs directly. This works well when your systems already have modern, well-documented APIs — Salesforce, HubSpot, Contentful, Stripe, most modern SaaS tools.

Pros: Simple, real-time, fewer moving parts.
Cons: Couples your AI app to each system's API. Rate limits and auth complexity multiply with each integration.

Pattern 2: Integration Layer (Middleware)

You build (or use) a middleware layer that normalizes data from multiple systems into a unified API that your AI app consumes. This is the right move when you have more than three or four systems to integrate, or when source systems have inconsistent data formats.

Pros: AI app stays clean. Swapping out a backend system doesn't require rewriting the AI layer.
Cons: More upfront work. Another system to maintain.

Pattern 3: Event-Driven (Async)

Systems publish events (order created, ticket updated, deal closed) to a message bus. The AI app subscribes to relevant events and reacts. This is the right pattern for workflows where real-time isn't critical but reliability is — think nightly report generation, lead enrichment, or anomaly detection.

Pros: Loose coupling. Systems don't need to know about each other. Scales well.
Cons: Eventual consistency. Debugging is harder when things go wrong.

In practice, most real-world AI integrations use a hybrid. Forrester's 2026 enterprise software predictions highlight that 30% of enterprise app vendors will launch their own MCP (Model Context Protocol) servers this year — a sign that the industry is converging on standardized ways for AI agents to interact with existing platforms through their APIs. The direction is clear: design for interoperability from day one.

Phase 3: Build the Data Pipeline First

Here's where most AI projects stall. The model is ready. The UI mockups look great. But the data pipeline — the plumbing that moves the right data from the right systems into the right format at the right time — isn't done.

For AI apps that integrate with existing systems, the data pipeline usually involves:

  1. Extraction: Pulling data from source systems. This sounds simple until you realize the CRM API paginates at 100 records, the ERP requires a custom SOAP call, and the internal tool only exports on Tuesdays.
  2. Transformation: Normalizing data formats, resolving entity mismatches (is "Acme Corp" in the CRM the same as "ACME Corporation" in the ERP?), and handling missing fields.
  3. Loading: Storing the processed data where the AI can use it — a vector database for RAG, a structured database for analytics, or both.
  4. Sync strategy: How often does data refresh? Real-time via webhooks? Batch every hour? Delta syncs? This decision affects cost, complexity, and how stale the AI's knowledge can get.

McKinsey's 2025 State of AI report found that only about one-third of companies have successfully scaled their AI programs — and the persistent blockers are fragmented data, legacy tech, and workflows that were never redesigned for AI. The data pipeline is where those blockers become concrete engineering problems you either solve or don't.

Phase 4: Choose the AI Layer Deliberately

Now — and only now — do you pick the AI components. With the integration architecture and data pipeline defined, the AI layer choices become much clearer.

Model Selection

Not every use case needs GPT-4 or Claude. Consider:

  • Classification and routing (e.g., ticket triage, lead scoring): A fine-tuned smaller model or even traditional ML may outperform an LLM at lower cost.
  • Text generation and summarization (e.g., report drafting, email composition): LLMs shine here. Use the best model you can afford for quality-sensitive outputs.
  • Structured data analysis (e.g., anomaly detection, forecasting): Statistical models or specialized ML, not language models.
  • Multi-step reasoning with tool use (e.g., "look up this customer, check their billing, draft a response"): Agent frameworks with tool-calling capabilities.

RAG vs. Fine-Tuning vs. Both

If your AI needs to know about your business data, you're choosing between retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), fine-tuning, or a combination. For most integration-focused AI apps, RAG is the right starting point — it lets you use your existing data without retraining a model, and it stays current as your data changes.

Fine-tuning makes sense when you need the model to behave in a specific way consistently (tone, format, domain-specific reasoning) and you have high-quality training examples.

Agent Orchestration

For AI apps that interact with multiple systems — pulling data from one, making decisions, and writing back to another — you need an orchestration layer. This is where frameworks like LangChain, CrewAI, or custom agent loops come in. The orchestration layer manages:

  • Which tools (APIs) the agent can call
  • What context it receives at each step
  • Error handling when an API call fails mid-workflow
  • Human-in-the-loop checkpoints for high-stakes decisions

Gartner predicts 40% of enterprise apps will feature task-specific AI agents by end of 2026, up from less than 5% in 2025. But Gartner also warns that over 40% of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by 2027 due to escalating costs and unclear value. The difference between the projects that survive and the ones that don't? Disciplined integration engineering — not flashier AI.

Phase 5: Build for Failure (Because Systems Fail)

This is the phase that separates production AI from demos. When your AI app integrates with five systems, the probability of at least one being unavailable at any given moment is surprisingly high. You need:

  • Graceful degradation. If the CRM API is down, the app should still function — maybe with cached data and a clear indicator that it's stale.
  • Retry logic with backoff. Rate-limited? Timed out? Don't hammer the API. Back off exponentially.
  • Circuit breakers. If a system is consistently failing, stop calling it and fall back to a default path rather than cascading failures through your entire app.
  • Observability. You need to see what the AI decided, what data it used, which APIs it called, and what went wrong — in real time. This isn't optional. When a stakeholder says "the AI gave the wrong answer," you need to trace exactly why in under five minutes.
  • Validation on writes. AI outputs that write back to existing systems (updating a CRM field, creating a ticket, posting to Slack) need validation layers. The AI might hallucinate a customer ID or generate an invalid status code. Catch it before it hits the system of record.

Deloitte's report found that only one in five companies has mature governance for autonomous AI agents. The governance gap is real, and it's most dangerous at the integration boundary — where AI decisions become system-of-record changes.

Phase 6: Ship, Measure, Iterate

Don't build the whole thing and then launch. Ship the smallest version that delivers value, put it in front of real users, and iterate.

A practical launch sequence:

  1. Week 1-2: Integration proof of concept — can you successfully read from and write to all required systems? This is the highest-risk phase. If the answer is no, you know early.
  2. Week 3-4: Core AI workflow with one integration. Pick the single most valuable data source and get the AI working end-to-end with it.
  3. Week 5-6: Add remaining integrations one at a time, testing each independently.
  4. Week 7-8: Observability, error handling, edge cases, user feedback loop.

This timeline assumes a team that knows what they're doing. The integration proof of concept in weeks 1-2 is the most important checkpoint — it surfaces 80% of the problems you'll face. If the APIs don't expose the data you need, or the auth model is incompatible, or the rate limits make your use case impractical, you want to know that before you've built the AI layer on top.

How We Approach This at Last Rev

We've been building integrated digital systems for years — composable architectures, headless CMS platforms, high-performance web applications that pull from multiple backends. AI integration is a natural extension of that work, and our approach reflects hard-won opinions:

  • Integration-first, AI-second. We start every AI engagement by mapping systems, APIs, and data flows. The AI layer is designed around what the integration architecture can reliably deliver, not the other way around.
  • API wrappers for everything. We don't let AI agents call raw third-party APIs directly. We build thin wrappers that handle auth, rate limiting, retries, and validation — so the AI layer stays focused on intelligence, not plumbing.
  • Observability is a launch requirement. No AI app goes live without comprehensive logging of every decision, every API call, and every data transformation. When something goes wrong (it will), we can trace the full chain in minutes.
  • Ship in weeks, not quarters. Our integration-first approach actually speeds delivery because it surfaces blockers early. The teams that spend three months building AI features only to discover an API limitation in month four — that's the timeline we help clients avoid.

The companies seeing real value from AI aren't the ones with the fanciest models. They're the ones that did the integration work properly — clean data pipelines, robust error handling, and AI that's designed to work within the constraints of real enterprise systems.

Key Takeaways

  • Start with systems, not models. Map every integration surface, data flow, and system owner before choosing any AI technology.
  • Pick the right integration pattern. Direct API, middleware, or event-driven — the choice depends on your system landscape, not the AI use case.
  • The data pipeline is the project. Extraction, transformation, sync strategy — this is where most AI integrations succeed or fail.
  • Choose AI components based on constraints. With the integration architecture defined, model selection, RAG vs. fine-tuning, and orchestration decisions become clearer.
  • Build for failure. Circuit breakers, graceful degradation, and observability are non-negotiable when your AI writes back to systems of record.
  • Ship the integration proof of concept first. Weeks 1-2 should answer: can we actually read and write to all required systems? Everything else builds on that answer.

The process isn't complicated. It's just disciplined. And discipline at the integration layer is what separates AI apps that run in production from AI apps that run in demos.
