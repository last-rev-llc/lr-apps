You've decided AI automation is worth the investment. Good. Now comes the harder part: picking the right agency to build it. Get this wrong and you'll join the 30% of generative AI projects that Gartner predicts will be abandoned after proof of concept — burning budget, burning time, and burning your team's trust in the technology.

The AI agency landscape is flooded. Every dev shop, consultancy, and freelancer has added "AI" to their website. Most of them are winging it. Here are the questions that separate serious partners from PowerPoint factories.

1. "What AI automations have you built that are still running in production?"

This is the single most important question. Not "what have you built?" — what's still running? Demos are easy. Production systems that handle edge cases, scale under load, and survive contact with real users are hard.

A good answer sounds like: "We built an automated client reporting pipeline for a professional services firm. It's been running for 8 months, processes 200+ reports weekly, and has a 94% accuracy rate with human review on the remaining 6%."

A bad answer sounds like: "We built a really cool chatbot prototype that impressed the board." Prototypes don't count. Press them for systems that have been in production for months, not weeks.

2. "What's your approach to AI that fails gracefully?"

AI systems are non-deterministic. They will produce unexpected outputs. The question isn't whether they'll fail — it's what happens when they do. According to McKinsey's 2025 State of AI report, only about one-third of organizations have successfully scaled AI across the enterprise. A huge reason: they didn't plan for failure.

You want to hear about guard rails, circuit breakers, human-in-the-loop escalation, and rollback strategies. If the agency talks only about the happy path, walk away. Every serious AI system needs a "what if the model is wrong?" plan for every automated action.

3. "How do you decide what to automate vs. what to augment?"

This question reveals whether the agency thinks strategically or just builds whatever you ask for. Not everything should be fully automated. Some workflows benefit from AI-assisted human decision-making rather than full automation.

The right answer involves a framework — something like: "We categorize tasks by risk level and judgment complexity. High-frequency, low-risk, low-judgment tasks get full automation. High-stakes decisions get AI augmentation with human approval." If they say "we can automate everything," that's a red flag. They're either overselling or they haven't hit enough edge cases yet.

4. "What does your data assessment look like before you start building?"

According to Gartner's 2025 research on AI-ready data, 63% of organizations either don't have or are unsure if they have the right data management practices for AI. An agency that jumps straight to building without assessing your data is setting you up for failure.

You want to hear about a discovery phase. What data do you have? Where does it live? How clean is it? What are the gaps? This should happen before any contract is signed, or at minimum as a paid discovery phase before the main build. Agencies that skip this step are guessing — and you're paying for their guesses.

5. "Who owns the models, the prompts, and the IP?"

This one bites people later. If the agency builds custom models, fine-tuned weights, or proprietary prompt chains — who owns them? What happens if the relationship ends?

Look for clear contractual terms on:

  • Code ownership: You should own the code they write for you. Period.
  • Prompt libraries: Are these yours or theirs? If they use the same prompts across clients, that's fine — but you should know.
  • Model weights: If they fine-tune a model on your data, you should own those weights.
  • Data: Your data stays yours. They shouldn't be training other clients' models on it.
  • Vendor lock-in: Can you take the system to another team if needed, or is it built on proprietary tools only they can maintain?

6. "How do you handle cost management and model economics?"

AI APIs aren't free. A poorly architected automation can burn through thousands in API costs monthly. The agency should have a clear perspective on:

  • Cost per automated task — and how to keep it predictable
  • When to use expensive models (GPT-4, Claude) vs. cheaper ones (GPT-4o-mini, open-source)
  • Caching strategies to avoid redundant API calls
  • Token optimization in prompts
  • When to invest in fine-tuning to reduce per-call costs at scale

If the agency can't tell you the approximate cost per transaction of the automations they're proposing, they haven't done this enough.

7. "What happens after launch? What does ongoing maintenance look like?"

AI systems aren't "build it and forget it." Models get updated, APIs change, data patterns shift, and prompts need tuning. This is fundamentally different from traditional software where the code does the same thing every time.

Ask specifically:

  • How do you monitor for model drift or accuracy degradation?
  • What's the process when an underlying model (GPT, Claude, etc.) releases a new version?
  • How often do prompts need tuning based on real-world performance?
  • What does your support/maintenance contract look like?

An honest agency will tell you that AI automation requires ongoing care. One that promises a "set it and forget it" solution is either lying or inexperienced.

8. "How do you approach security and compliance for AI systems?"

This matters more than most buyers realize. When AI systems process business data, you need to understand:

  • Data residency: Where is your data being sent? Which AI providers? What are their data retention policies?
  • PII handling: If automations touch customer data, how is PII stripped or protected?
  • Audit trails: Can you trace every AI decision back to its inputs and reasoning?
  • Access controls: Who (and what) can trigger automated actions?

The agency should be able to articulate a clear security posture for AI — not just recite their cloud provider's compliance certifications. If you're in a regulated industry (finance, healthcare, legal), they need specific experience with those constraints.

9. "Can you show me how you measure ROI on AI automation?"

Vague promises of "efficiency gains" aren't enough. A competent agency should help you define measurable outcomes before building anything:

  • Hours saved per week on specific tasks
  • Error rate reduction (with before/after measurement)
  • Revenue impact (faster response times, more throughput)
  • Cost savings (reduced manual labor, fewer mistakes to fix)

The best agencies build measurement into the system itself — dashboards that show exactly what the automation is doing and how it's performing against baseline. If they can't quantify the value of what they're building, why are you building it?

10. "What would you tell us NOT to automate?"

This is the trust question. An agency that's genuinely good at this will push back on some of your ideas. They'll tell you that certain processes aren't good candidates for AI — maybe the volume is too low to justify the investment, maybe the task requires too much nuanced judgment, or maybe a simple rule-based automation would work better than an AI-powered one.

If they say yes to everything, they're optimizing for their revenue, not your results.

Red Flags to Watch For

Beyond these questions, watch out for these warning signs during the evaluation process:

  • "We use a proprietary AI platform." Translation: vendor lock-in. You want solutions built on open standards and widely-adopted tools.
  • No discovery phase. If they'll quote you a fixed price without understanding your systems, data, and workflows — they're guessing.
  • All demos, no production references. Ask to speak with clients whose AI automations have been running for 6+ months.
  • They can't explain the technology simply. If they hide behind jargon and buzzwords, they may not understand it well enough to build it reliably.
  • No mention of testing or evaluation. AI systems need rigorous testing — not just unit tests, but evaluation frameworks that measure output quality over time.

The Bottom Line

Hiring an AI automation agency is a significant decision. The technology is powerful but immature, the vendor landscape is chaotic, and the cost of a bad engagement is high — not just in dollars, but in organizational trust.

The right agency will feel more like a strategic partner than a vendor. They'll push back on bad ideas, be transparent about limitations, and build systems designed to evolve as the technology improves. Use these questions to find them.
